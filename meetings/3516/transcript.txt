JAMES FLATTUM: Good evening, everyone. This is James Flattum from Greater Denver Transit. My
comments tonight are going to focus on the operator shortage, what is perhaps the single largest
challenge RTD faces today. RTD management and staff have been very consistent, this is the number
one problem preventing RTD from restoring service and increasing frequencies.
Looking back, the operator shortage probably goes back to before the pandemic. It is certain that
pandemic conditions certainly exacerbated it, with labor shortages across the country. As you would
expect, when we look at other transit peer agencies around the country, we see operator shortages have
impacted them as well. What is not universal, however, is the recovery.
Several of RTD's peer agencies have made significantly more progress in staffing back up. In March of
2022, RTD raised standard hourly wages from $2.58 to $24 for starting operators. In July of last year,
RTD's combined full-time and part-time bus and light rail operator count numbered 911 people. Today this
number is 930 people. So despite having a full year at this higher wage, RTD has only achieved a net
increase of 19 operators over 12 months.
As an example, compare this to LA Metro. LA Metro announced it had hired 1,000 new bus operators
between February, 2022 and May of 2023. There were certainly offsets with some workforce attrition not
included in this number, but the increase net was sufficient for LA Metro to increase their frequencies to
pre-pandemic levels and restore cancellation rates to their pre-pandemic averages. LA Metro also raised
pay, but only to $23, which is $1 lower than RTD, in a city that has higher costs of living.
So their success cannot be attributed to easier operating conditions either. LA Metro serves a larger
population than RTD and LA is a city that is widely known for experiencing severe social challenges with
homelessness, drug abuse, and poverty that are orders of magnitude beyond what Denver faces today.
We at GDT are not going to hold RTD responsible for the macro conditions of the pandemic. However,
the question for management and the Board is, what is RTD doing? What does RTD have in their control
that is not sufficiently working to attract and retain operators? What else can RTD do to make the job
experience more attractive?
We've done some cursory digging ourselves, speaking with RTD operators, DTO operators, Allied
Security staff, and we have a few points to recommend for the Board to look into. One, questions have
been raised about whether health care and 401(k) benefits are sufficiently competitive for operators. Two,
certain operators and staff have reported they may not get the basic dignities, such as lunch breaks,
which may otherwise be possible. And three, finally, we've been given accounts of lower middle
managerial staff who are known for hostile personalities that may be contributing to excessive attrition
and may lack the leadership skills for their task.
Whatever the primary cause is, the lack of progress around hiring clearly deserves scrutiny from the
Board, as it is hampering service quality more than any other thing at RTD. We would urgently push for a
rapid review and peer agency benchmarking analysis to ascertain best practices among peer agencies
for hiring and retention. Thank you. That concludes my comments.

BRETT PAGLIERI: Hi. Thank you. My name is Brett Paglieri. I am a Edgewater resident who uses and
enjoys RTD. I would like to see RTD modernize their decision making processes to be data driven in
order to provide better routing service. It is now the standard that route planning and ridership models are
based on origin and destination data from riders and modeled using modern data sciences. RTD should
strive to know where people start and end their journeys. And most of that information could be available
from our existing infrastructure with changes, but isn't being utilized to its fullest potential.
Transfer information is important and feasibly obtainable from the current validation system, but we have
access to so much more in this digital age. Apps like Google and Apple maps collect millions of queries
and journeys in Denver every day and know what percentage of those end up being real trips. Third-party
apps, like the Transit app, have real time GPS tracking for people taking transit, along with enormous
amounts of data showing where people searched to go.

To me, as someone in the Silicon Valley world, there's no valid excuse for not having ridership data in an
age when my phone, my watch, and even my car log and track information everywhere I go. Data
crucially can show us what trips aren't being taken. Having data is knowing the why, why people choose
other options besides RTD. RTD should follow the lead of others and update the app to obtain more data
and mine its data to find out best places to put their routes and forecast future ridership.
I advocate that you use data to drive better service and ridership. For example, the RTD app signs me out
and makes me log back in after inactivity. Even Facebook keeps you logged in because they know a
small percentage of people will be turned away from any obstacle between them and their service.
Thank you for helping to make things better and I look forward to seeing a modern, data driven, efficient
transportation service that strives to be at the forefront of modern practices and technology. I have high
hopes for RTD for being a world class transit service, constantly improving upon itself by using data and
best practices. Data would show you more people who ride if we allowed you to tap a credit card to pay,
where and how to improve wayfinding, and how to invest in service to improve more lives.
I would like to see RTD follow the lead of others and use data, as it's already being used to make
decisions, especially so we can catch up with the rest of the country. Thank you very much.

LYNN GUISSINGER: Thank you so much. The second report is from the Advisory Committee for People
with Disabilities, or ACPD. Mr. Earl Lee, please begin.
EARL LEE: Thank you, Madam Chair. The ACPD meeting was held on September 6. Gabe Christie
introduced four new members to the group. We have also just today decided on two more members that
we are going to bring on and hoping by the beginning of 2024 that we will have full membership for the
ACPD committee.
The main discussion that we carried on the 6TH, we continued our ongoing discussion of elevator
reliability, especially in locations that do not have an alternative method of access, such as a ramp. In
these areas, an operating elevator is not a matter of convenience but a necessity for people who use
wheelchairs and other mobility devices. It's become a real problem for a number of people.
Several members echoed their concerns throughout the system, where they have not been able to
access an elevator or they get off of a train, in particular, and find out that an elevator is not available,
they can't get down working up to the levels that they need to and they have no idea what to do from that
point.
The one person who could speak to these issues unfortunately was not available to attend. So at this
point, we are looking forward to a process that apparently is being worked on, being developed by RTD,
and we will continue those discussions in our next meeting.
Audio messages was the next discussion that was brought up, the next topic. Often, the audio messages
are garbled and unintelligible, especially on train platforms and buses, the fixed route buses. There's
inconsistent volumes and poor audio quality. The audio announcements are often not effective in helping
passengers find their stops. This is especially true of the blind, who rely heavily on auditory messages.
Shawn Spears, ex officio member, has had several reports from blind students using Route 66 that the
bus announcements simply are inefficient, or insufficient. We also discussed briefly other possible

technologies, NFC, near-field communications, some other things that we feel need to start getting looked
at as alternatives to simply audio.
The subject of ramp width on the free MallRide buses was brought up. One member saw a gentleman
with a wider wheelchair who could barely get on and off of the bus because the sides of his wheels were
scraping the edge of the ramp. This is very dangerous because it would be very easy for that person to
go up over the ramp and fall.
Now we realize that this is not something that can be corrected today, but we want to make sure that this
is something that is considered in the future as new equipment is beginning to be spec'ed for both fixed
route and for mall shuttle buses.
Nick Rorres, who works for RTD, was quick to say that the MallRide ramps are 30.5 inches, which is
wider than the fixed route bus ramps, and that both meet the ADA standards. And I think what this tells us
is that while it may be the ADA minimum standard, what we're finding today is that some of the ADA
standards that were written so long ago don't meet the real world challenges that we face today.
Finally, we got a presentation from Andrea Zuckerman on the new website refresh that she and her team
are working on. She did send out a survey for input from the ACPD committee members on how they
currently use the website and what would make it better for them in the future. So we're looking forward to
how that impacts the direction that she takes the website.
And that concludes my report for this evening, and thank you very much.

appropriations prior to October 1, 2023. The federal Antideficiency Act generally bars the obligation or
expenditure of federal funds in the absence of appropriations. As a result, Congress must enact a
continuing resolution, as we commonly refer to as a CR, to provide interim budgetary authority and
prevent a government shutdown.
If a funding gap occurs, federal agencies are generally required to begin a shutdown of affected projects
and activities, which includes the prompt furlough of non-excepted personnel. This means that Congress
must enact a CR this week to prevent a government shutdown beginning Sunday, October 1.

impacts of TSA workers and things of the like, we had to forego that this Friday, when I received a call
from the Deputy TSA administrator. But that just shows the critical aspects of everything that is happening
right now.
It is unclear whether Congress will reach a bipartisan, bicameral agreement to prevent a shutdown. The
impacts of the government shutdown on the USDOT agencies vary considerably. The Chair made
reference to FTA, recognizing that operations will continue and none of its 700 employees will be
furloughed, given that FTA operations are financed by either the Highway Trust Fund or advance
appropriations under the bipartisan infrastructure law. The FTA will be able to reimburse transit agencies,
as we heard from the Chair, for previously approved projects. However, it is not clear whether new grants
will be approved.
Additionally, to the extent that FTA activities involve other agencies, the personnel of those agencies,
such as the Environmental Protection Agency, US Department of Labor, and US Department of the
Interior may be furloughed, which could stop progress on projects. And recognizing that we do operate
commuter rail, I would be remiss not to reference the Federal Railroad Administration. 350 employees, or
35%, would be furloughed during a government shutdown.
Remaining FRA personnel will be focused primarily on activities necessary to protect life and property,
such as safety inspections. The FRA would not continue any of its unfunded core agency function nor
contracts, purchase orders, or other documents obligating funds would be executed, nor would the

agency be able to advance any rulemakings, including those related to railroad safety. Similarly, 455
employees, or 25% of the Office of the Secretary of Transportation, would also be furloughed. The Board
will be apprised of any anticipated impacts to RTD in the wake of a federal government shutdown.
